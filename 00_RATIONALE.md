# Research rationale

Automatic speech-quality predictors are normally validated by their agreement with listener ratings, while ASR data-selection pipelines more often filter transcription confidence, alignment, or domain relevance. The literature therefore leaves a consequential question insufficiently tested: whether a *globally comparable* no-reference quality score is a valid ranking of an utterance's utility for training ASR at a fixed audio budget. This is especially doubtful because MOS labels and their learned predictors are context-sensitive across corpora and conditions, and a recent synthetic-data study found no clear relation between NISQA/intelligibility scores and downstream ASR performance. I hypothesize that global top-$k$ quality filtering preferentially removes acoustically useful variation and that this loss of source/condition coverage can erase or reverse any benefit from higher predicted quality. The proposed paper tests this hypothesis with controlled raw-audio corruptions and an external natural-speech validation: it compares DNSMOS and reference-free SQUIM selection against random and diagnostic oracle policies at matched duration and optimization budgets, then tests a source-quantile policy that ranks utterances only within their recording source and targets each source's duration share to segment granularity. The central claim to be evaluated is deliberately narrow: *quality scores may be useful within source, but their uncalibrated global ordering should not be assumed to measure downstream ASR training utility.*
